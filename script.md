SLIDE 28: LIFECYCLE STAGES IN INKLUTRACK

InkluTrack organizes the PWD development journey into six distinct lifecycle stages, each with specific system functionality.

Enrollment creates the client profile, capturing demographic information, disability details, and initial documentation. Assessment establishes behavioral and skills baselines—the reference points against which all progress will be measured.

Training is the longest stage, involving continuous progress tracking against defined milestones and productivity indicators. The system records behavioral observations, skill development, and participation metrics.

Evaluation applies AI-based prediction to analyze accumulated data. Are outcomes on track? What interventions might help? Which employment opportunities align with demonstrated capabilities?

Placement activates job matching features, connecting client profiles with suitable opportunities. The system recommends positions based on skills, training outcomes, and behavioral readiness.

Retention continues tracking after employment, monitoring job retention and long-term success. This data feeds back to improve predictions and recommendations for future clients.

Stage transitions can be automated based on defined criteria or manually triggered by staff when professional judgment warrants it. Every transition is documented for continuity.

SLIDE 29: AI FEATURES DETAILED

Let me detail InkluTrack's two primary AI features.

The Productivity Prediction Engine analyzes historical data combined with current metrics to identify patterns indicating risk. The process flows from data collection through pattern analysis to risk scoring, ultimately generating intervention suggestions.

What does this mean practically? If a client's attendance begins declining while behavioral incidents increase, the system recognizes this pattern and alerts staff. It doesn't just flag the problem—it suggests when intervention is most likely to be effective, based on patterns observed across the client population.

The Job Matching Algorithm operates differently. It builds a capability matrix from the client's profile, skills assessment results, and documented training outcomes. This matrix is then compared against job opportunity requirements to generate alignment scores. The output is a ranked list of recommendations, prioritized by match quality and augmented by behavioral readiness considerations.

Importantly, these AI features SUPPORT human decision-making—they don't replace it. Staff review all recommendations and retain full authority over interventions and placements.

Regarding generalizability: our findings will be specific to the AVRC context. The framework should be adaptable to similar rehabilitation settings, but further research would be needed to validate broader application.
